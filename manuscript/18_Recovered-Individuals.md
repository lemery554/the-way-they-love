# Chapter 18

## Recovered Individuals

Sarah was already dressed when Brooke came downstairs.

Not pajamas. Work clothes. A navy blouse, black pants, earrings small enough for video calls. She stood at the kitchen island answering email while Claire packed lunches neither of them had been told they were taking anywhere.

"You're going to school," Sarah said.

Brooke stopped on the last stair. "Yesterday you wouldn't let me."

"Yesterday you were exhausted."

"And today?"

"Today you need routine."

The decision had been made before Brooke entered the room. Sarah's laptop was open to an email from Ashcroft High with the subject line *RECOVERY, ATTENDANCE, AND FAMILY SUPPORT*. Brooke could read only the first sentence from where she stood.

*As our community moves forward together...*

"You emailed them again," Brooke said.

"They emailed everyone."

"I mean about us."

Sarah closed the laptop. "I told them you've received attention online and may need support."

"What kind of support?"

"The kind where adults know strangers have identified your school."

Brooke looked at Claire. "Did you tell her?"

"It was in the comments," Claire said.

"You were reading my comments?"

"They were about us."

Sarah put both hands on the island. "A reporter called the house at six thirty. Someone sent the school a link. Two parents complained that your videos are frightening their children. This is no longer only your account."

"It was never only my account. That's the point."

"No. The point is that you showed our home, your sister, the hospital, and me. Now people know where you go to school."

Every fact was true. Sarah did not have to raise her voice to make truth feel like a locked door.

"Give me my phone," Brooke said.

"It's charging."

"Where?"

"In my room."

Brooke stared at her.

Sarah's expression remained patient. "It kept going off all night. You need one morning without strangers demanding answers."

"You took it while I was asleep."

"I put it somewhere quiet."

"That is taking it."

Claire slid a lunch bag across the counter. "You can have mine."

"I don't want yours."

"Brooke," Sarah said.

"No. Don't do the voice like I'm making this hard. Give me my phone."

Sarah held her gaze for a moment, then went upstairs. She returned with the phone and placed it on the island rather than in Brooke's hand.

The screen showed thirty-seven missed notifications, three interview requests, a message from the assistant principal, and a warning from the video platform. The warning did not remove the upload. It said the account's medical claims were under review and future posts could have their reach limited.

At the top of the screen was an incoming call from an unknown Connecticut number.

Sarah looked at it. "That's probably another reporter."

Brooke declined the call.

"Thank you."

"I didn't do it for you."

"I know."

Sarah said it without anger. That made Brooke want to argue more.

On the television behind them, a morning anchor interviewed a state health official. The caption called people like Claire and Sarah *recovered individuals*. The official explained that post-illness distress could be worsened by abrupt isolation and recommended calm routines, familiar support, and avoiding "unnecessary family separation."

Brooke opened the school email on her phone. The counselor's message used the same phrase.

The platform warning used *supportive recovery information*.

Sarah's employer had sent *Supporting Recovered Family Members at Home*.

Different offices. Different logos. The same handful of words.

At breakfast Sarah had also said she was expected back at the office the next morning. Her supervisor wanted recovered employees onsite for a short check-in before they resumed normal schedules. Sarah had sounded pleased by the idea. The house would be empty for the first time since Claire became sick, except for Cooper.

"Where did the school get this?" Brooke asked.

Sarah glanced at the television. "The health department, probably."

It was an ordinary answer. That was what frightened her.

Brooke went upstairs with her phone. Claire followed as far as the landing.

"I need to record," Brooke said.

"I can be quiet."

"I need the door closed."

Claire's eyes moved to the new bathroom handle visible down the hall. Sarah had replaced it with one that did not lock.

"For how long?"

"Ten minutes."

"Five."

"Ten."

Claire looked downstairs. Sarah was on a work call, her voice carrying from the kitchen.

"Seven," Claire said.

Brooke almost refused because negotiating privacy made it no longer privacy. Instead she said, "Fine."

Claire sat against the wall outside the bedroom. Brooke closed the door.

She recorded Upload Six in one take.

"They say we're fine," she began. "Not just my family. The hospital, the school, the news. They keep using the same words because they're all getting the same guidance, and maybe that started for a good reason. But the guidance doesn't match what's happening in houses."

She showed the platform warning, the school email, and the hospital notice without displaying names or addresses. She described Sarah's recovery but did not include the nighttime recording. That remained in the chemistry folder and on the camera card.

"My mom is working. My sister goes to school. They eat and sleep and remember everything. That doesn't mean nothing happened to them."

Brooke stopped. Outside the door, Claire shifted against the wall.

"People keep asking what to do. I don't know. I know dogs notice. I know closed doors upset them. I know they can remember something exactly and still make it mean something different. I know treating every request for space like cruelty makes it easier for them to keep you close."

She looked directly into the lens.

"If somebody says they feel safer when you can't leave, that isn't the same as you being safe."

Brooke ended the recording at six minutes and forty-one seconds.

Claire was still sitting outside.

"You said them," she said.

"I said people who recovered."

"You said them."

Brooke opened the door wider. "What should I say?"

"Our names."

"I have."

"Then say them again."

Brooke did not answer. Claire stood and brushed lint from Brooke's flannel.

"Mom says we have to leave in four minutes."

"You counted?"

"You said seven."

At school, the assistant principal met them before first period.

Mr. Dorsey had always looked uncomfortable around students, as if teenagers were an audience he had not prepared for. That morning he held his office door open and asked Brooke and Claire to sit together on the small couch.

Brooke chose the chair.

"We've had some calls," he said.

"From reporters?"

"One reporter. Several parents. A local account reposted part of your video with the school name."

"I didn't give them the name."

"I understand."

"Do you?"

Mr. Dorsey folded his hands. "Our concern is student safety and minimizing disruption. We also want to avoid language that stigmatizes students who have recovered."

Claire sat at the end of the couch, watching Brooke.

"She broke a bathroom lock," Brooke said.

"At home?"

"Does it matter?"

"It affects what the school can address."

"She's left class to find me more than once."

Mr. Dorsey turned to Claire. "Is that true?"

"Yes."

"Why?"

"I wanted to know where she was."

He nodded with the solemnity of someone receiving a reasonable explanation. "Given what your family has been through, reassurance is understandable. But you still need permission to leave class."

"Okay," Claire said.

Brooke laughed once. "That's the whole response?"

"No. We can make a support plan. Scheduled check-ins. Shared lunch period if teachers approve. A counselor available to both of you."

Every solution gave Claire more official access.

"I don't want a shared lunch."

"Then we won't begin there," Mr. Dorsey said. "But Brooke, the school cannot treat your sister as dangerous based on videos and family conflict."

"The videos are evidence."

"They're edited."

The word landed harder than it should have. Brooke had said the same thing about other people's clips. Editing did not make something false, but it meant someone had chosen what remained.

Mr. Dorsey slid a printed page across the desk. The heading read *TEMPORARY SEPARATION DISTRESS: SCHOOL SUPPORT GUIDANCE*.

"The counselor will speak with you both," he said.

Brooke left the paper on his desk.

By lunch, three students had asked whether Claire's eyes really glowed. One asked Brooke to film him pretending to vomit red medicine. A sophomore she did not know whispered, "My dad does it too," then walked away before Brooke could ask what she meant.

Her sixth upload passed ten thousand views before the final bell. The top reply said:

*Your mother is trying to protect you. Stop turning love into evidence.*

Brooke saved the comment.

At home, Sarah stood inside the front door waiting.

"You're eleven minutes late."

"Mr. Dorsey kept me."

"You could have called."

"You had my phone half the morning."

"And then I gave it back."

Claire entered behind Brooke. Sarah touched her shoulder, then Brooke's, counting them by contact.

"I worry when I don't know where you are," Sarah said.

The sentence could have belonged to any mother. Brooke heard Claire's voice beneath it anyway.

Upstairs, she opened her public archive to check what strangers could see. Hundreds of old videos waited beneath the outbreak uploads: school projects, Cooper as a puppy, Claire ruining takes, Mark fixing lights, Sarah calling everyone to dinner.

Brooke chose one from three years earlier and scrubbed through the foyer shot. Claire entered the front-door code while Brooke narrated something about composition. The keypad flashed blue at the edge of the frame.

The digits were visible.

Brooke paused, zoomed in, and watched Claire's hand enter the code again.

She set the video to private.

Then she began checking the others.
