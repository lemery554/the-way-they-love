# Chapter 11: Frame by Frame

Brooke showed Sarah the marks in the morning.

She had planned the conversation in the shower. She would be calm. She would show the timestamped photograph and the yellowing ovals still visible inside her wrist. Facts were harder to argue with. That was why she recorded things.

Sarah stood at the counter with her coffee and read a health-department alert aloud. Recovery rates were above ninety percent statewide. Updated guidance replaced *isolation* with *support at home* and encouraged families to provide reassurance after acute symptoms ended.

"Claire grabbed me last night," Brooke said. "Hard."

Sarah looked at the marks. "She almost died on Sunday. Now she wants to hold on to her sister."

"I told her she was hurting me."

"And she let go."

"After Cooper barked."

Sarah set her phone facedown. "I know you're frightened. I was there when she was sick. But you have decided something about Claire, and now you're collecting evidence for it. She is not a project."

"I'm writing down what happens."

"You're deciding what it means." Sarah picked up the phone again. "It's unkind, Brooke. She adores you, and you're treating her like she's contagious."

The word found the cruel sentence Brooke had used on Claire before the fever. Maybe the list was not evidence. Maybe it was an alibi, a way to make the sister she had hurt into something that deserved suspicion.

Brooke nearly accepted that because it was the kinder explanation.

Then she went upstairs and opened the footage.

She arranged the clips by date and watched them once for picture and once for sound. Most contained exactly what Sarah would have seen: humming, following, a shoulder held too close. A camera did not care what Brooke feared. Its indifference began to feel like a verdict.

She found the frame in a twelve-second hallway clip from two nights earlier. Claire stood at the dark end of the landing, looking toward the stairs while Cooper remained below. When Claire lifted her face, her eyes caught the weak streetlight and returned a greenish shine.

Brooke stopped.

One frame later, it was gone.

She moved backward, then forward. The color appeared only once. It was not a glow. It could have been the sensor forcing detail out of darkness, a passing car, or the ordinary reflection cameras sometimes pulled from animal eyes and occasionally human ones at the wrong angle. Brooke exported the frame anyway.

She searched the other clips and found nothing she trusted. Twice she thought she saw the same color, but enlarging the image broke it into compression blocks. Green became gray when she changed the monitor brightness. A shape at the edge of Claire's pupil vanished when Brooke stopped wanting it to be there.

One frame was not proof.

That evening she tried to reproduce it.

She left the overhead light off and set her phone on the shelf facing the bed. The monitor gave the room a weak blue cast. When she opened the door, Claire appeared within a minute and sat at the edge of the mattress.

"You're up late," Claire said.

"So are you."

"I couldn't sleep."

Brooke asked about school and a show they used to watch. She kept Claire talking while the phone recorded, then asked her to move closer to the window because the light was better there.

"Are you filming me?"

The question stopped Brooke.

"The room."

Claire looked toward the shelf. "Am I in it?"

"Yes."

"Why?"

Brooke could have said she was testing the camera. That would have been true. Instead she reached up and stopped the recording.

"I wanted to check something."

"About me?"

Brooke did not answer quickly enough.

Claire's face changed, not into the flat calm Brooke feared but into ordinary hurt. "You said I get a veto."

"You do."

"After you start?"

Brooke lowered the phone. "I'm sorry."

Claire stood. For a moment Brooke expected her to refuse the apology or demand that Brooke follow. Claire only said, "Delete it."

Brooke deleted the clip in front of her. Claire watched the screen until it disappeared, then left the room without asking Brooke to come.

The failed experiment should have made Brooke feel better. It did not. She had used Claire's need for her as bait and learned nothing except that Claire still understood a promise Brooke had broken.

Later, while clearing accidental recordings, Brooke opened a forty-second clip of her empty bedroom from the previous afternoon. Traffic moved outside. The house settled. Then Claire entered.

She crossed to the desk and looked at the dark laptop. She sat on the edge of Brooke's bed, hands flat on her knees, facing the doorway.

Near the end, she spoke.

The waveform barely rose. Brooke put in an earbud and increased the volume.

"Wait."

Or perhaps it was the beginning of *wait for me*, cut short when the recording ended. It might have been directed at Brooke downstairs, a memory of something she meant to say, or no word at all. The consonant frayed inside the room noise.

Brooke listened once more. The more she replayed it, the less certain it became.

Downstairs, Claire laughed at something Sarah said. The sound was warm and familiar.

Brooke considered showing Sarah the frame and the audio. She could already hear the reasonable answers: cameras invented color in darkness; a girl alone in her sister's room might say her sister's name, or tell her to wait, or talk to herself. Sarah would be right about the evidence even if she was wrong about Claire.

Brooke saved the original hallway clip and the accidental bedroom recording. She did not label either as proof. In *Things I Have Seen*, she added:

*One frame reflected green. Could be the camera.*

*She may have said wait when she thought she was alone. Audio unclear.*

She closed the laptop when the stairs went quiet. The frame had not answered anything. It had only given uncertainty a color.
